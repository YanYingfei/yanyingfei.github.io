## 0. Intuition

Lattice-based folding follows a similar methodology: 
- start from linear relations
- norm check
- (linear combination) fold $\to$ decompose
	-  or decompose $\to$ fold

### 1. Linear relation

**1. Row-tensor structure.** 
- $$\mathbf{F}$$ can be written as $$\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{F}_0 \bullet \ldots \bullet \mathbf{F}_{\mu-1}$$, where $$\mathbf{F}_i \in \mathcal{R}_q^{n \times d}$$ for $i \in [\mu]$ and $\bullet$ denotes the row-wise tensor product, by the shorthand $$\mathbf{F} \in \mathcal{R}_q^{n \times d^{\otimes\mu}}$$.

Concretely, the row-wise tensor product means that for each row index $j \in [n]$, the $j$-th row of $\mathbf{F}$ is the tensor product of the $j$-th rows of $$\mathbf{F}_0, \ldots, \mathbf{F}_{\mu-1}$$. 

- For example
$$
\mathbf{F}_0 = \begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 \\
3 & 4
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf{F}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}
5 & 6 \\
7 & 8
\end{bmatrix}.
$$
	Then $\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{F}_0 \bullet \mathbf{F}_1 \in \mathcal{R}_q^{2 \times 4}$ is obtained row by row:
$$
\begin{aligned}
	\mathbf{f}_0 &= (1,2) \otimes (5,6) = (5,6,10,12), \\
	\mathbf{f}_1 &= (3,4) \otimes (7,8) = (21,24,28,32).
\end{aligned}
$$
$$
\mathbf{F} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{f}_0 \\ \mathbf{f}_1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}
5 & 6 & 10 & 12 \\
21 & 24 & 28 & 32
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

**2. vSIS assumption.** 

The (vanishing-)vSIS assumption states that, for a random row-tensor $\mathbf{F} \leftarrow_{\$} \mathcal{R}_q^{n \times d^{\otimes\mu}}$, it is hard to find a short vector $\mathbf{x}$ satisfying $\mathbf{Fx} = \mathbf{0} \bmod q$. 
Based on this assumption, for a short vector $\mathbf{w}$, the vector $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{Fw} \bmod q$ is a computationally binding commitment (similar to Ajtai commitment). 

Depending on the tensor structure of $\mathbf{F}$, the product $\mathbf{Fw}$ can be seen as the evaluation of a polynomial with coefficients given by $\mathbf{w}$. 
- For example, if $m = 2^\mu$ and $\mathbf{F} = (1, f, \dots, f^{m-1}) = (1, f^{m/2}) \otimes (1, f^{m/4}) \otimes \ldots \otimes (1, f)$, then $\mathbf{Fw} = \sum_{i=0}^{m-1} w_i f^i$. 

**3. Principal linear relation.** 

$$
\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n},n,m,r,\beta} := \begin{cases}
((\mathbf{H}, \mathbf{F}, \mathbf{Y}), \mathbf{W}) \\
\mathbf{H} \in \mathcal{R}_q^{\hat{n} \times n} \quad \mathbf{F} \in \mathcal{R}_q^{n \times m} \\
\mathbf{Y} \in \mathcal{R}_q^{\hat{n} \times r} \quad \mathbf{W} \in \mathcal{R}^{m \times r} \\
\|\mathbf{W}\|_{\sigma,2} \leq \beta \quad \text{and} \quad \mathbf{HFW} = \mathbf{Y} \bmod q
\end{cases}
$$

where $\mathbf{H}$ is restricted to take the form $\mathbf{H} = \begin{bmatrix}  ~\mathbf{I}_{\hat{n}} & \underline{\mathbf{H}}~  \end{bmatrix}$ for some $\hat{n} \leq n$. Throughout, we think of $n = \bar{n} + \underline{n}$ and $\mathbf{F} = \begin{bmatrix} ~\bar{\mathbf{F}}~ \\ ~\underline{\mathbf{F}}~ \end{bmatrix} \in \mathcal{R}_q^{(\bar{n}+\underline{n}) \times m}$ as consisting of a top part $\bar{\mathbf{F}} \in \mathcal{R}_q^{\bar{n} \times m}$ and a bottom part $\underline{\mathbf{F}} \in \mathcal{R}_q^{\underline{n} \times m}$. 
- the top rows $\bar{\mathbf{F}}$ for the vSIS **commitment**;
- the bottom rows $\underline{\mathbf{F}}$ for **polynomial evaluations**.

Compare with the main relation in Latticefold+ [1, 2] (Commit + Linear relation)

$$%
\mathcal{R}_{lin,B} = \left\{ (\mathbb{i,x,w})
\middle|
\begin{aligned}
&\mathbb{x} = (\mathsf{cm}_\mathbf{f}, \mathbf{r}, \mathbf{v}_1, ..., \mathbf{v}_{n_{lin}}),\\
&\mathbb{w} = \mathbf{f},\\
&\mathsf{cm}_{\mathbf{f}} =
\mathsf{Com}(\mathbf{f}),\\
&\Vert \mathbf{f} \Vert_\infty < B,\\
& \forall i \in [n_{lin}],\; \langle \mathbf{M}^{(i)} \mathbf{f}, \mathsf{tensor}(\mathbf{r}) \rangle =\mathbf{v}_i
\end{aligned}
\right\}$$

### 2. Folding summary

A brief summary of the functionalities of these RoKs:
1. $\Pi^{\mathsf{join}}$ joins two claims together. 
2. $\Pi^{\mathsf{norm}}$ implements an exact norm-check.
3. $\Pi^{\mathsf{RP}}$ and $\Pi^{\otimes\mathsf{RP}}$ are approximate norm checks via (un)structured random projections.
4. $\Pi^{\mathsf{fold}}$ folds $\mathbf{W} \in \mathcal{R}^{m \times r}$ and reduces $r$ to $1$, i.e. $\mathbf{W} \to \mathbf{w} \in \mathcal{R}^{m}$
5. $\Pi^{b\text{-decomp}}$ $b$-ary decomposes $\mathbf{W}$, reducing the norm bound $\beta$ but increasing the witness dimension $r$.


The composition of the RoKs for the folding scheme consists of the following six individual RoKs:

$$
\Pi^{\mathsf{join}} \rightarrow \Pi^{\mathsf{norm}} \rightarrow \Pi^{\otimes\mathsf{RP}} \rightarrow \begin{matrix} \Pi^{\mathsf{fold}} \\ \to \end{matrix} \rightarrow \Pi^{\mathsf{join}} \rightarrow \Pi^{\mathsf{batch}} \rightarrow \Pi^{b\text{-decomp}}.
$$

The chain of the reduction is prefixed with an additional $\Pi^{\mathsf{join}}$ RoK, which merges the multiple instances into a single relation. 

- Start with the relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n}, n, \mu, r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L, \beta}$$ where $L$ is the number of instances to be folded. 
- Then norm check
	- Precise norm check: The first RoK $\Pi^{\mathsf{norm}}$ takes as input the relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n}, n, \mu, r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L, \beta}$$ and outputs the relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n} + 2, n + 2, \mu, r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L, \beta}$$. 
	- Approximate norm check: Next, the protocol $$\Pi^{\otimes\mathsf{RP}}$$ takes as input the relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n} + 2, n + 2, \mu, r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L, \beta}$$ and outputs two relations: $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n} + 3, n + 3, m, r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L, \beta}$$ and $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\bar{n} + 1, \bar{n} + 1, m, 1, \hat{\beta}}$$  where $$\hat{\beta} = m_{\mathsf{rp}} \cdot \beta$$ and $$m_{\mathsf{rp}} = \mathcal{O}(\lambda)$$. 
- Next fold
	- The next RoK $\Pi^{\mathsf{fold}}$ takes the first relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n} + 3, n + 3, m, r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L, \beta}$$ and outputs the relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n} + 3, n + 3, m, 1, (r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L)\gamma\beta}$$ where $\gamma$ is an expansion factor of the challenge set. 
	- Two branches are then merged using the RoK $$\Pi^{\mathsf{join}}$$, resulting in the relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n} + 4, n + 4, m, 2, \max(\hat{\beta}, (r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L)\gamma\beta)}$$. 
- Last decompose
	- Eventually, the last two RoKs $\Pi^{\mathsf{batch}}$ and $\Pi^{b\text{-decomp}}$ take as input the relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n} + 4, n + 4, m, 2, \max(\hat{\beta}, (r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L)\gamma\beta)}$$ and output the relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n}, n + 4, m, 2\ell, \beta}$$ as $\ell$ is set so that decomposition of the witness of norm bounded by $$\max(\hat{\beta}, (r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L)\gamma\beta)$$ results in a witness of norm bounded by $\beta$.

## 3. The protocols
### 3.1 $\Pi^{\mathsf{join}}$ : merge many instances into one [4]

- input: $L$ instances  of  relation $\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n}, n, m, 1, \beta}$ and one relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n}, n, m, r_{acc}, \beta}$$  (Old folded relations)
- output: relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n}, n, m, r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L, \beta}$$
This requires sending cross-terms of the bottom rows of $\mathbf{F}$ corresponding to different instances. 
As a result, the number of rows of $\mathbf{F}$ increases by $(n - \bar{n}) \cdot (L - 1)$ where $L$ is the number of instances to be folded.

- output:

$$\Xi_{\hat{n},n,\mu,\tilde{\mu},r,\beta,2}^{\text{lde}(-\otimes)} := \left\{ \begin{array}{l} ((\mathbf{H}, \mathbf{F}, \mathbf{Y}, (\mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{s}_i, \mathbf{M}_i)_{i \in [2]}), \mathbf{W}): \\ \quad ((\mathbf{H}, \mathbf{F}, \mathbf{Y}), \mathbf{W}) \in \Xi_{\hat{n},n,\mu,r,\beta}^{\text{lin}} \\ \quad (\mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{s}_i, \mathbf{M}_i)_{i \in [2]} \in (\mathcal{R}_q^{\tilde{\mu}} \times \mathcal{R}_q^r \times \mathcal{R}_q^{\tilde{m} \times m})^2 \\ \quad \forall_{i \in [2]} \quad \mathbf{s}_i = \text{LDE}[\mathbf{M}_i \mathbf{W}](\mathbf{r}_i) \bmod q \end{array} \right\}$$

By embedding $\mathbf{s_1, s_2}$ into $\mathbf{HFW=Y}$, we get the relation:
$$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n}+2, n+2, m, r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L, \beta}.$$

### 3.3 $\Pi^{\otimes\mathsf{RP}}$ : Approximate range check [5]

#### 3.3.1 Previous results

1. **subtractive sets**. A set $C \subset \mathcal{R}$ is called subtractive if for any two distinct elements $c,c' \in C$, the inverse of $c-c'$ exists in the ring and has low norm. 

	- subtractive sets in knowledge soundness 
	after extracting two accepting transcripts with challenges $c \neq c'$, one often recovers the witness in the form

$$\mathbf{w} := \frac{\mathbf{z}-\mathbf{z}'}{c-c'},$$

where $\mathbf{z},\mathbf{z}'$ are short vectors from the transcripts. 

- The soundness error of this methodology is essentially $1/\|C\|$, so ideally one would like $C$ to be very large. 

- However, known subtractive sets in the relevant rings have only polynomial size in the security parameter. As a result, one needs many repetitions to drive the soundness error down to negligible, which increases the proof size.

2. **Johnson--Lindenstrauss Random Projection**

	- Motivation: To use an exponential-size unstructured challenge set and get one-shot soundness, reducing $r$ to $O(1)$ and dropping communication to $O(1)$ ring elements per round.
	
	- Johnson--Lindenstrauss Random Projection
	the verifier samples a random small integer matrix $\mathbf{J}$, say $\{-1, 0, 1\}$, and the prover returns the projected image
$$
\mathbf{v} := \mathbf{J}\,\mathsf{cf}(\mathbf{w}) \bmod q,
$$
	where $\mathrm{cf}(\mathbf{w})$ is the coefficient vector of the ring elements. 
	The verifier then checks that $\mathbf{v}$ has low norm, and the prover proves the well-formedness of the above equation.
	
- The correctness: with high probability over the choice of $\mathbf{J}$, the projection approximately preserves norm, so $\|\mathbf{v}\| \approx \|\mathbf{w}\|$. 
- Knowledge soundness, if an extracted candidate witness actually had large norm, then except with exponentially small probability in the projection dimension, its projection would also have large norm.

#### 3.3.2 The protocol $\otimes \mathsf{RP}$ [5]

$\otimes\mathsf{RP} \to$ apply tensor production on the random project.

**Setup:** The prover holds a witness $\mathbf{w}$ satisfying $\mathbf{Fw} = \mathbf{y}$ with $\|\mathbf{w}\| \leq \beta$.

**Step 1 — Sample the projection matrix.**
The verifier samples a random matrix $\mathbf{J}' \in \mathcal{R}^{n_\mathsf{rp} \times m_\mathsf{rp}}$ with entries from some small integer distribution $\chi$ . The full projection is block-diagonal: $\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{I} \otimes \mathbf{J}'$.

**Step 2 — Compute the projected witness.**
The prover computes $\hat{\mathbf{w}} := \mathbf{J}\mathbf{w} \bmod q$, st. $||\mathbf{w} || \approx ||\mathbf{J}\mathbf{w}||$ ,then the prover
- sends out $\hat{\mathbf{w}}$ directly, the verifier checks its norm.
- Alternative: commits to $\hat{\mathbf{w}}$ to reduce its size: $\hat{\mathbf{F}} \hat{\mathbf{w}} = \hat{\mathbf{z}}$.

**Step 3 — Produce two linked claims.**
$$\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}\tilde{\mathbf{F}} \\ \mathbf{I}\otimes\mathbf{J}'\end{bmatrix}\mathbf{w} = \begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{y} \\ \hat{\mathbf{w}}\end{bmatrix}}_{\text{claim on original } \mathbf{w}} \qquad \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}\hat{\mathbf{F}} \\ \mathbf{I}\end{bmatrix}\hat{\mathbf{w}} = \begin{bmatrix}\hat{\mathbf{z}} \\ \mathbf{w}\end{bmatrix}}_{\text{claim on projected } \tilde{\mathbf{w}}}$$
The shared $\mathbf{w}$ cryptographically ties the two together — a cheating prover cannot swap in a different $\tilde{\mathbf{w}}$ without breaking both claims simultaneously.

- input: relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n}+2, n+2, m, r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L, \beta}.$$
- output: $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n} + 3, n + 3, m, r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L, \beta}$$ and $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\bar{n} + 1, \bar{n} + 1, m, 1, \hat{\beta}}$$ where $\hat{\beta} = m_{\mathsf{rp}} \cdot \beta$ and $m_{\mathsf{rp}} = \mathcal{O}(\lambda)$. 
### 3.4 Fold  

#### 3.4.1 Fold the witness [4]

- input: the first relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n} + 3, n + 3, m, r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L, \beta}$$ 
- outputs the relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n} + 3, n + 3, m, 1, (r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L)\gamma\beta}$$ 

The fold protocol $\Pi^{\mathsf{fold}}$ reduces the witness width $r$ to $1$ (by random linear combining the columns).

#### 3.4.2 Merge the relations from $\Pi^{\mathsf{RP}}$, $\Pi^{\mathsf{Fold}}$

- input: the relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\bar{n} + 1, \bar{n} + 1, m, 1, \hat{\beta}}$$ from $\otimes \mathsf{RP}$, and the relation $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n} + 3, n + 3, m, 1, (r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L)\gamma\beta}$$ from $\mathsf{Fold}$$.
- output:  $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n} + 4, n + 4, m, 2, \max(\hat{\beta}, (r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L)\gamma\beta)}$$
- $\bar{\mathbf{F}}$ (dimension $\bar{n}$) is shared, thus $\mathbf{H} \in \mathcal{R}^{(\hat{n} + 4) \times (n + 4)}$ and $\mathbf{F} \in \mathcal{R}^{({n} + 4) \times m}$.

### 3.5 Batch and Decompose

### 3.5.1 Batch the statement [4]

- Input: $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n} + 4, n + 4, m, 2, \max(\hat{\beta}, (r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L)\gamma\beta)}$$
- Output: $$\Xi^{\mathsf{lin}}_{\hat{n}, n + 4, m, 2, \max(\hat{\beta}, (r_{\mathsf{acc}} + L)\gamma\beta)}$$ 
	- in the following figure, the challenge $\mathbf{c}^\top$ is a row, thus $$\mathbf{H} \in \mathcal{R}^{(\hat{n} + 4) \times (n + 4)}$$ is reduced to $$\tilde{H}\in \mathcal{R}^{(\bar{n} + 1) \times (n + 4)}$$ (top-part + 1-row)
	- pick $\mathbf{c}^\top$ to be $(\hat{n}-\bar{n})$-row to get  $$\mathbf{H} \in \mathcal{R}^{\hat{n} \times (n + 4)}$$, i.e., our output relation.

### 3.5.2 b-ary Decomposition [4]

The $b$-ary decomposition protocol $\Pi^{b\text{-decomp}}$ reduces the witness norm $\beta$ by $b$-ary decomposing the matrix $\mathbf{W}$ as $\sum_{i=0}^{\ell-1} b^i \mathbf{V}_i$, at the expense of increased width $\widetilde{r}$ in the resulting $\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}$. 
The prover needs to communicate $$\mathbf{Y}_i = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{F}\mathbf{V}_i$$. 
The new witness is $$\widetilde{\mathbf{W}} = (\mathbf{V}_0, \dots, \mathbf{V}_{\ell-1})$$ and the resulting $$\widetilde{\mathbf{Y}}$ is $(\mathbf{Y}_0, \dots, \mathbf{Y}_{\ell-1})$$.
